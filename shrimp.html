WHAT CAN MANTIS SHRIMP SEE? by Me

Question: The mantis shrimp can perceive both polarized light and multispectral images; they have the most complex eyes in the animal kingdom. Human eyes have color receptors for three colors (red, green, and blue); the mantis shrimp has receptors for sixteen types of color, enabling them to see a spectrum far beyond the capacity of the human brain. Seriously, how cool is the mantis shrimp: mantisshrimp.uchicago.edu What might they be able to see that we cannot? What are we missing?

Interestingly, the three color receptors humans possess happen to coincide with the number of dimensions in space, so therefore humans can be considered 3-dimensional beings of both space and color. Under this definition, mantis shrimps would be living in a 16-dimensional world of vision. Considering that human beings cannot grasp higher dimensional space, we likewise cannot grasp the world of the mantis shrimp. We can never understand the vision of the world of the mantis shrimp because that's beyond our comprehension limits. Thus, whether its world is shining with iridescent light, filled with heavenly rainbows or not, we will never know.

If we inspect deeper though, visible light, like other electromagnetic waves, really have no characteristics of so-called "color". All perceptions of colors are actually transmuted, edited by the brain to form our vision. To this extent human and mantis shrimp are similar--both of us are actually seeing what our brain shows us. Hence seeing becomes a subjective matter--nothing to do with number of receptors but with how the brain interprets the signals the receptors receive.

Even if the mantis shrimp can really see a much more wonderful world than ours, it may not possess the ability to interpret much of it.

Humans may have a visual system not as good as that of the shrimp's, but we are gifted with the mind's ability to attach emotions and memories to visual stimulants. By laying my eyes on the old wall of Florence, I was able to connect with history and "see" the old magnificent days of Italy, kings and bishops walking down the marble stairs. The scene evoked a sense of awe yet melancholy--all the grandiose, all the vicissitudes, were all annihilated by time, only walls remains. Seeing is not about eyes, it's about mind and heart.

The savant effect of psychology states that when you discard your vision, you gain something in the other aspect. Our powerful minds compensate for our "lack" of vision; we do not miss anything.

I once read a story from the NATGEO magazine written by an astronomer, demonstrating the profound extents of our senses, and I believe it serves as a perfect conclusion: "The sound of crunching rocks startled me. A few feet away three wild horses, black on black against the nightscape, wandered past. They never glanced skyward at the gossamer swath of Hale-Bopp nor at the wondrous spectacle that is the night sky on a clear night, comet or no.

It is good to be human."
